# Residual Risk Framework

## Overview

The Residual Risk Framework defines the governance process used to evaluate, document, approve, monitor, and report risks that remain after remediation efforts have been completed.

The purpose of this framework is to:

- Establish a consistent residual risk governance process
- Support informed risk acceptance decisions
- Improve executive visibility into unresolved exposure
- Ensure compensating controls are documented
- Strengthen audit defensibility
- Maintain traceability between risk treatment and risk acceptance
- Support enterprise governance maturity

Residual risk governance ensures risks that cannot be fully eliminated remain visible and formally managed.

---

# What Is Residual Risk?

Residual Risk is the remaining level of risk that exists after controls, remediation activities, and mitigation efforts have been implemented.

Residual Risk may exist because:

- Technical limitations prevent full remediation
- Business requirements prevent control implementation
- Cost exceeds remediation benefit
- Legacy systems cannot support modern controls
- Operational constraints exist
- Vendor dependencies remain unresolved
- Leadership chooses to accept the remaining exposure

Residual Risk does not mean a risk is ignored.

Residual Risk means:
- exposure is understood
- exposure is documented
- exposure is approved
- exposure is monitored

---

# Residual Risk Governance Principles

The Residual Risk Framework operates using the following principles:

| Principle | Description |
|---|---|
| Visibility | Accepted risks remain visible within governance reporting. |
| Accountability | Ownership remains assigned even after acceptance. |
| Traceability | Acceptance decisions are documented and auditable. |
| Reviewability | Residual risks are periodically reassessed. |
| Executive Oversight | Significant residual risks require leadership approval. |
| Compensating Controls | Alternative controls should be implemented where feasible. |
| Audit Defensibility | Risk acceptance decisions must be supported by evidence and rationale. |

---

# Residual Risk Lifecycle

Residual Risk governance follows the lifecycle below:

1. Risk Identification
2. Risk Assessment
3. Remediation Planning
4. Remediation Execution
5. Validation Review
6. Residual Risk Evaluation
7. Risk Acceptance Review
8. Executive Approval
9. Ongoing Monitoring
10. Periodic Reassessment

---

# Residual Risk Evaluation Criteria

Residual risks should be evaluated using:

- Current Risk Score
- Remaining Business Impact
- Remaining Regulatory Exposure
- Remaining Operational Exposure
- Existing Compensating Controls
- Control Maturity
- Cost of Additional Remediation
- Business Justification

---

# Residual Risk Decision Matrix

## Option 1: Accept

The organization formally accepts the remaining exposure.

Typical conditions:

- Further remediation is not feasible
- Business value outweighs remaining risk
- Exposure falls within organizational risk tolerance
- Executive approval obtained

---

## Option 2: Mitigate Further

Additional remediation activities are required.

Typical conditions:

- Exposure remains above risk tolerance
- Additional controls are feasible
- Cost of remediation is justified

---

## Option 3: Transfer

Risk is transferred to another party.

Examples:

- Cyber insurance
- Contractual risk transfer
- Vendor responsibility agreements

---

## Option 4: Avoid

The activity creating the risk is discontinued.

Examples:

- Decommissioning a vulnerable application
- Ending a high-risk vendor relationship
- Removing unsupported technology

# Residual Risk Approval Matrix

| Risk Severity | Approval Authority |
|---|---|
| Low | Department Manager |
| Moderate | Director or Business Unit Leader |
| High | Executive Leadership |
| High with Regulatory Exposure | Executive Leadership + Compliance Review |

---

# Required Residual Risk Documentation

Every residual risk decision must include:

- Risk ID
- Risk Title
- Original Risk Score
- Current Risk Score
- Control Maturity Assessment
- Compensating Controls
- Business Justification
- Acceptance Decision
- Approval Authority
- Approval Date
- Review Date
- Assigned Owner

---

# Compensating Controls

When risks cannot be fully remediated, compensating controls should be evaluated.

Examples include:

- Increased monitoring
- Enhanced logging
- Manual review procedures
- Additional approvals
- Segmentation controls
- Security awareness training
- Vendor oversight activities

Compensating controls should be documented and periodically validated.

| Field | Description |
|---|---|

| Risk Owner | Assigned accountable owner |
| Original Risk Score | Initial assessed risk |
| Residual Risk Score | Current remaining risk |

| Acceptance Status | Accepted, Pending, Rejected |
| Approval Authority | Individual approving acceptance |

| Next Review Date | Scheduled reassessment date |

---

# Monitoring & Reassessment

Accepted risks remain subject to governance oversight.

Review frequency should be based on severity.

| Severity | Review Frequency |
|---|---|
| Low | Annually |
| Moderate | Quarterly |
| High | Monthly |

Residual risk reviews should assess:

- Changes in business context
- Changes in threat landscape
- Control effectiveness
- Regulatory changes
- New remediation opportunities

---

# Executive Reporting

Residual Risk reporting should provide visibility into:

- Open accepted risks
- High residual risks
- Risks approaching review dates
- Risks requiring re-approval
- Compensating control effectiveness
- Trends in accepted risk exposure

Residual risks should remain visible within executive dashboards and governance reporting.

---

# Audit Readiness Considerations

Residual Risk decisions must be defensible during:

- Internal audits
- External audits
- Regulatory examinations
- Compliance reviews
- Customer assessments

Documentation should clearly demonstrate:

- Why remediation was not completed
- What compensating controls exist
- Who approved acceptance
- When the risk will be reviewed again

# Summary

The Residual Risk Framework provides a structured approach for governing risks that remain after remediation efforts have been completed.

By requiring documentation, approval, monitoring, and periodic reassessment, the organization ensures accepted risks remain visible, accountable, and aligned with enterprise risk tolerance.
