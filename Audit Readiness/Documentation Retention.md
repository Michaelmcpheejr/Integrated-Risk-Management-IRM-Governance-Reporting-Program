# Documentation Retention Framework

## Overview

The Documentation Retention Framework establishes the governance requirements for the retention, protection, storage, review, and disposal of governance, risk, compliance, audit, vendor management, and security-related records throughout the Integrated Risk Management (IRM) Governance & Reporting Program.

The purpose of documentation retention is to:

- Support audit readiness
- Demonstrate regulatory compliance
- Preserve governance history
- Maintain evidence availability
- Support legal and contractual obligations
- Improve organizational accountability
- Enable historical reporting and trend analysis
- Strengthen governance defensibility

Documentation retention ensures that governance records remain available, accurate, and accessible throughout their required lifecycle.

---

# Governance Philosophy

Governance activities generate organizational records.

Examples include:

- Risk Assessments
- Risk Registers
- Audit Reports
- Vendor Assessments
- Policy Exceptions
- Remediation Records
- Governance Reviews
- Executive Reports

Without proper retention practices:

- Audit evidence may be lost
- Compliance obligations may not be met
- Governance decisions cannot be validated
- Historical risk trends cannot be analyzed

The objective of retention is to preserve organizational knowledge while supporting regulatory and business requirements.

# Documentation Retention Schedule

The organization should maintain minimum retention periods for governance records.

| Record Type | Minimum Retention Period |
|----------|----------|
| Risk Assessments | 7 Years |
| Risk Register Records | 7 Years |
| Audit Reports | 7 Years |
| Audit Evidence | 7 Years |
| Governance Review Records | 7 Years |
| Vendor Assessments | Contract Term + 7 Years |
| Vendor Risk Acceptances | 7 Years |
| Policy Exceptions | 5 Years |
| Compliance Assessments | 7 Years |
| Executive Reports | 7 Years |
| Meeting Minutes | 5 Years |
| Remediation Records | 7 Years |

Retention periods may be extended when required by regulatory, contractual, legal, or litigation hold requirements.

# Documentation Ownership

Each record should have an assigned owner.

| Role | Responsibility |
|----------|----------|
| Document Owner | Maintains document accuracy |
| GRC Team | Oversees governance records |
| Compliance Team | Reviews compliance documentation |
| Internal Audit | Validates audit-related records |
| Executive Leadership | Reviews strategic governance documentation |

Ownership improves accountability and record quality.

---

# Storage Requirements

Governance documentation should be stored in approved repositories.

Storage solutions should:

- Restrict unauthorized access
- Maintain document integrity
- Support version control
- Maintain audit logs
- Support backup and recovery

Documentation should not be stored in unmanaged locations.

---

# Documentation Classification

Documents should be classified according to sensitivity.

Examples:

| Classification | Example Records |
|----------|----------|
| Public | Published policies |
| Internal | Governance procedures |
| Confidential | Risk assessments |
| Restricted | Audit evidence, regulatory investigations |

Classification should determine handling requirements.

---

# Version Control Requirements

Governance records should maintain:

- Version history
- Approval history
- Change records
- Effective dates
- Review dates

Version control improves audit defensibility and traceability.

---

# Documentation Review Requirements

Documentation should be reviewed periodically.

| Record Type | Review Frequency |
|----------|----------|
| Policies | Annual |
| Procedures | Annual |
| Risk Methodologies | Annual |
| Governance Frameworks | Annual |
| Vendor Governance Documentation | Annual |
| Compliance Documentation | Annual |

Reviews should ensure documentation remains current and effective.

---

# Archiving Requirements

When records are no longer actively used:

- Move records to approved archive repositories
- Maintain retrieval capability
- Preserve integrity and access controls
- Retain metadata and ownership information

Archived records should remain searchable and auditable.

---

# Documentation Disposal Requirements

At the end of the retention period:

1. Verify retention requirements have been met
2. Confirm no legal hold exists
3. Obtain approval for disposal
4. Securely destroy records
5. Document disposal activity

Disposal should be performed in a manner that prevents unauthorized recovery.

---

# Legal Hold Requirements

Documentation subject to:

- Litigation
- Investigations
- Regulatory inquiries
- Audit requests

should be preserved regardless of retention schedules.

Legal hold requirements supersede standard disposal procedures.

---

# Documentation Retrieval Requirements

Governance records should be retrievable within defined service levels.

Recommended targets:

| Record Type | Retrieval Target |
|----------|----------|
| Active Records | Same Business Day |
| Archived Records | Within 3 Business Days |
| Audit Requests | Within 24 Hours |

Rapid retrieval supports audit readiness.

# Summary

The Documentation Retention Framework establishes a structured approach for managing governance, risk, compliance, audit, and vendor management records throughout their lifecycle.

By defining retention requirements, ownership responsibilities, storage controls, review procedures, archiving standards, and disposal requirements, the organization strengthens audit readiness, regulatory compliance, governance maturity, and enterprise accountability.
