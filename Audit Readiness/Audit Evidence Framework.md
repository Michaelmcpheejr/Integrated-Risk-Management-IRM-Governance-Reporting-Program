# Audit Evidence Framework

## Overview

The Audit Evidence Framework establishes the governance process used to identify, collect, validate, retain, and present evidence supporting the operation of governance, risk, compliance, security, and audit-related controls throughout the Integrated Risk Management (IRM) Governance & Reporting Program.

The purpose of this framework is to:

- Support audit readiness
- Demonstrate control effectiveness
- Improve governance traceability
- Strengthen regulatory compliance
- Standardize evidence collection practices
- Support internal and external audits
- Improve defensibility of governance decisions
- Maintain historical records of control operation

Evidence serves as the foundation for validating that controls are operating as intended and that governance activities are being performed consistently.

---

# Governance Philosophy

If an activity cannot be proven through evidence, it cannot be reliably validated.

Examples:

- Access reviews
- Risk assessments
- Vendor assessments
- Remediation activities
- Policy approvals
- Security monitoring
- Governance reviews

While activities may occur operationally, governance programs require evidence demonstrating that activities were completed, reviewed, approved, and validated.

The objective is not simply to perform controls.

The objective is to demonstrate control effectiveness through verifiable evidence.

---

# Audit Evidence Objectives

The Audit Evidence Program seeks to:

- Demonstrate control operation
- Support audit testing
- Improve governance accountability
- Establish traceability
- Support regulatory obligations
- Improve remediation validation
- Strengthen governance maturity

# Evidence Lifecycle

All evidence should follow a standardized lifecycle.

```text
Control Activity Performed
            ↓
Evidence Generated
            ↓
Evidence Collected
            ↓
Evidence Validated
            ↓
Evidence Stored
            ↓
Audit Review
            ↓
Evidence Retention
            ↓
Evidence Disposal
```

# Evidence Quality Standards

All evidence should meet the following standards.

## Accuracy

Evidence must accurately reflect the activity performed.

---

## Completeness

Evidence should contain sufficient information to support review and validation.

---

## Timeliness

Evidence should be collected as close as possible to the activity date.

---

## Traceability

Evidence should be traceable to:

- Risk
- Control
- Issue
- Finding
- Remediation Activity
- Policy Requirement

---

## Integrity

Evidence should be protected from unauthorized modification.

---

## Accessibility

Authorized personnel should be able to retrieve evidence when needed.

---

# Evidence Collection Requirements

Evidence collection should occur during normal governance operations.

Examples:

| Activity | Evidence Collected |
|----------|----------|
| Risk Assessment | Completed Risk Assessment Record |
| Vendor Assessment | Assessment Documentation |
| Remediation Activity | Remediation Validation Evidence |
| Policy Review | Approval Record |
| Governance Meeting | Meeting Minutes |

Evidence should be collected proactively rather than during audits.

---

# Evidence Validation Process

Evidence should be reviewed before being accepted into governance records.

Validation should confirm:

- Evidence is authentic
- Evidence is complete
- Evidence supports the activity claimed
- Evidence aligns with control requirements
- Evidence meets governance standards

---

# Evidence Reliability Hierarchy

Not all evidence carries equal weight.

## High Reliability

Examples:

- System-generated logs
- Automated reports
- Audit reports
- Configuration exports
- Security monitoring outputs

Preferred during audits.

---

## Moderate Reliability

Examples:

- Approved procedures
- Meeting minutes
- Management attestations
- Risk assessments

Useful when supplemented with additional evidence.

---

## Low Reliability

Examples:

- Verbal confirmations
- Informal communications
- Unverified screenshots

Should not serve as sole evidence source for critical controls.

---

# Evidence Ownership Model

| Role | Responsibility |
|----------|----------|
| Control Owner | Generates and maintains evidence |
| Risk Owner | Reviews evidence supporting risks |
| GRC Team | Oversees evidence governance |
| Audit Team | Validates evidence during reviews |
| Executive Leadership | Reviews evidence for strategic decisions when required |

---

# Evidence Mapping Requirements

Evidence should be linked whenever possible to:

```text
Framework Requirement
        ↓
Policy
        ↓
Control
        ↓
Risk
        ↓
Assessment
        ↓
Evidence
```

This improves governance traceability and audit defensibility.

# Evidence Review Frequency

| Evidence Type | Review Frequency |
|----------|----------|
| Critical Controls | Quarterly |
| High-Risk Controls | Quarterly |

# Audit Readiness Indicators

The organization should monitor:

- Evidence Collection Completion Rate
- Missing Evidence Rate
- Audit Request Fulfillment Time
- Validation Success Rate
- Evidence Quality Findings

These indicators help measure governance maturity.

---

# Governance Reporting

Evidence reporting should provide visibility into:

- Missing evidence
- Evidence validation failures
- Audit support activities
- Outstanding audit requests
- Evidence collection performance

---

# Evidence Security Requirements

Evidence repositories should:

- Restrict unauthorized access
- Maintain integrity controls
- Support version history
- Maintain audit logs
- Follow retention requirements

Evidence should be treated as sensitive governance records.

# Summary

The Audit Evidence Framework establishes a structured and repeatable approach for collecting, validating, managing, and retaining evidence supporting governance, risk, compliance, audit, and security activities.

By ensuring evidence is accurate, complete, traceable, and readily available, the organization strengthens audit readiness, governance maturity, regulatory compliance, and enterprise accountability.
