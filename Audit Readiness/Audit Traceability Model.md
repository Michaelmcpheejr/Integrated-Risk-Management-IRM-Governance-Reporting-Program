# Audit Traceability Model

## Overview

The Audit Traceability Model establishes the governance framework used to create, maintain, validate, and demonstrate traceability across risks, controls, policies, issues, findings, remediation activities, evidence, and governance decisions throughout the Integrated Risk Management (IRM) Governance & Reporting Program.

The purpose of traceability is to:

- Improve audit readiness
- Strengthen governance visibility
- Support regulatory compliance
- Improve accountability
- Demonstrate control effectiveness
- Support remediation tracking
- Enable end-to-end governance oversight
- Improve executive reporting accuracy

Traceability ensures every governance activity can be linked to a business objective, control requirement, risk exposure, and supporting evidence.

---

# Governance Philosophy

One of the most common audit questions is:

> "How do you know this control addresses this risk?"

Or:

> "Show me how this finding was remediated."

Organizations that lack traceability often struggle to:

- Demonstrate control effectiveness
- Validate remediation activities
- Support audits
- Explain governance decisions
- Prove compliance

The purpose of traceability is to establish clear relationships between governance artifacts.

Every governance activity should be connected to a business purpose.

---

# Traceability Objectives

The Audit Traceability Program seeks to:

- Link risks to controls
- Link controls to policies
- Link findings to risks
- Link remediation to findings
- Link evidence to control activities
- Link governance decisions to risk outcomes
- Improve reporting consistency
- Support audit defensibility

---

# Enterprise Traceability Architecture

The organization utilizes a centralized governance relationship model.

```text
Business Objective
          ↓
Policy
          ↓
Control
          ↓
Risk
          ↓
Assessment
          ↓
Issue / Finding
          ↓
Remediation
          ↓
Validation
          ↓
Evidence
          ↓
Closure
```

This structure enables complete lifecycle visibility.

---

# Primary Governance Relationships

## Policy → Control

Policies define governance requirements.

Controls enforce those requirements.

Example:

```text
Access Control Policy
          ↓
Quarterly Access Review Control
```

Purpose:

Demonstrate policy implementation.

---

## Control → Risk

Controls exist to mitigate risks.

Example:

```text
Quarterly Access Review
          ↓
Excessive Privilege Risk
```

Purpose:

Demonstrate risk mitigation.

---

## Risk → Assessment

Assessments evaluate risk exposure.

Example:

```text
Privileged Access Risk
          ↓
Access Governance Assessment
```

Purpose:

Measure risk posture.

---

## Assessment → Finding

Assessments may identify deficiencies.

Example:

```text
Access Governance Assessment
          ↓
Missing Quarterly Reviews
```

Purpose:

Identify governance gaps.

---

## Finding → Remediation

Findings generate corrective actions.

Example:

```text
Missing Quarterly Reviews
          ↓
Implement Review Process
```

Purpose:

Track resolution efforts.

---

## Remediation → Validation

Completed remediation requires validation.

Example:

```text
Implement Review Process
          ↓
Review Evidence Validation
```

Purpose:

Confirm effectiveness.

---

## Validation → Evidence

Validation must be supported by evidence.

Example:

```text
Review Validation
          ↓
Access Review Reports
```

Purpose:

Demonstrate remediation success.

# Risk Traceability Model

Every risk should maintain relationships to:

| Governance Element | Purpose |
|----------|----------|
| Policy | Requirement being supported |
| Control | Mitigation mechanism |
| Owner | Accountability |
| Assessment | Evaluation activity |
| Findings | Identified deficiencies |
| Remediation | Corrective actions |
| Evidence | Proof of operation |

---

# Control Traceability Model

Every control should maintain relationships to:

| Governance Element | Purpose |
|----------|----------|
| Policy | Governing requirement |
| Risk | Risk mitigated |
| Control Owner | Accountability |
| Assessment | Validation mechanism |
| Evidence | Operational proof |

---

# Issue Traceability Model

Every issue should maintain relationships to:

| Governance Element | Purpose |
|----------|----------|
| Assessment | Source of issue |
| Risk | Associated exposure |
| Control | Control deficiency |
| Remediation | Resolution activity |
| Validation | Verification activity |

# Executive Traceability Requirements

Executive reporting should provide visibility into:

- Risk ownership
- Open findings
- Remediation status
- Control effectiveness
- Vendor governance
- Policy exceptions

Leadership should be able to trace any significant issue to its root cause.

---

# Traceability Validation Process

Periodic validation should confirm:

- Relationships remain accurate
- Required links exist
- Ownership is assigned
- Evidence is available
- Remediation activities remain current

Missing relationships should be treated as governance deficiencies.

---

| Controls Mapped to Policies | Measures compliance alignment |
| Findings with Remediation Plans | Measures accountability |

# Audit Readiness Benefits

Strong traceability enables organizations to:

- Respond to audit requests quickly
- Demonstrate governance maturity
- Improve evidence collection
- Strengthen compliance reporting
- Reduce audit preparation effort
- Improve executive visibility

Traceability reduces reliance on manual investigation during audits.

# Summary

The Audit Traceability Model establishes a structured framework for linking policies, controls, risks, assessments, findings, remediation activities, validation efforts, and evidence into a unified governance ecosystem.

By maintaining end-to-end traceability, the organization improves audit readiness, governance visibility, compliance defensibility, and executive decision-making while supporting enterprise risk management maturity.
