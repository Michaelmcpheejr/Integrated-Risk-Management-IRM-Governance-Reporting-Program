# Continuous Monitoring Model

## Overview

The Continuous Monitoring Model establishes the governance framework used to maintain ongoing visibility into third-party risk throughout the vendor lifecycle.

The purpose of continuous monitoring is to:

- Detect changes in vendor risk posture
- Identify emerging threats and exposures
- Improve third-party governance visibility
- Support proactive risk management
- Strengthen regulatory compliance oversight
- Reduce supply chain risk
- Improve executive awareness of vendor-related risks

Continuous monitoring ensures vendor risk assessments remain current rather than becoming point-in-time evaluations.

---

# Governance Philosophy

Vendor risk is not static.

A vendor that presents acceptable risk today may present unacceptable risk tomorrow due to:

- Security incidents
- Regulatory actions
- Financial instability
- Control failures
- Organizational changes
- Technology changes
- New threat activity

Continuous monitoring enables the organization to identify risk changes as they occur and respond appropriately.

The objective is not simply to assess vendors.

The objective is to maintain ongoing awareness of third-party risk exposure.

---

# Monitoring Objectives

The Continuous Monitoring Program seeks to:

- Identify significant vendor risk changes
- Detect vendor security incidents
- Monitor remediation activities
- Track compliance status
- Support reassessment decisions
- Improve governance reporting
- Enable timely risk escalation

# Monitoring Frequency Model

Monitoring frequency should align with vendor tier and risk exposure.

| Vendor Tier | Monitoring Frequency |
|---|---|
| Tier 1 (Critical) | Continuous Monitoring |
| Tier 2 (High-Risk) | Quarterly |
| Tier 3 (Moderate-Risk) | Semi-Annual |
| Tier 4 (Low-Risk) | Annual |

Higher-risk vendors require greater governance visibility.

# Reassessment Triggers

A formal vendor reassessment should occur when:

- Significant security incident occurs
- Major control failure identified
- Regulatory exposure changes
- Vendor acquires new access privileges
- Significant service expansion occurs
- Critical remediation efforts fail
- Vendor risk exceeds tolerance

Not all monitoring events require reassessment.

Governance review should determine reassessment necessity.

---

# Vendor Risk Escalation Criteria

Monitoring activities should trigger escalation when:

- Tier 1 vendor experiences security incident
- Vendor risk exceeds tolerance
- Regulatory exposure increases
- Critical remediation activities fail
- Business continuity concerns emerge
- Vendor controls become ineffective

Escalation should follow the Escalation Procedures Framework.

# Monitoring Documentation Requirements

Monitoring activities should document:

- Monitoring Date
- Vendor Name
- Vendor Tier
- Event Identified
- Risk Impact
- Recommended Action
- Escalation Status
- Reassessment Requirement

Monitoring records should be retained for governance and audit purposes.

---

# Executive Reporting

Executive reporting should focus on:

- Tier 1 Vendors
- High-Risk Vendors
- Vendor Security Incidents
- Vendor Risk Acceptance Decisions
- Open Critical Findings
- Significant Vendor Risk Changes

The objective is to provide leadership with visibility into third-party risk exposure across the organization.

# Summary

The Continuous Monitoring Model establishes a structured governance framework for maintaining visibility into third-party risk after vendor onboarding and approval.

By monitoring security incidents, compliance status, remediation activities, operational performance, financial stability, and emerging threats, the organization improves third-party oversight, strengthens governance maturity, and reduces supply chain risk exposure throughout the vendor lifecycle.
